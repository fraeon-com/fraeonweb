No Man's Sky: The Basics

Posted: 4/2/2016

What is No Man's Sky

No Man's Sky finally has a release date and I can’t wait to start exploring the universe that the team over at Hello Games has set in motion. To get ready for the launch of one of the most anticipated games of the last few years we’ll be taking a closer look at everything No Man’s Sky has in store for us.

While there has been tons of coverage for the title during it’s development Sean Murray, the creator of No Man’s Sky, and his team over at Hello Games have been keeping quiet about most details about the game. As Murray has explained they would rather have people experience the game and all its secrets than find everything out before they’ve had a chance to play. However there is a lot to learn from the many articles, videos, and interviews around the web and we’ll be pouring through them to find every secret they hold.

Starting with the basics though, No Man’s Sky is a space sim/exploration/survival game set in a huge procedurally generated sci-fi universe with over 18 quintillion planets, each of which you can fly to in your own spaceship, land on the surface and explore every open field, mountain, river, ocean, cave, and forest. All the while discovering and naming new creatures, finding alien relics, stumbling into alien cities and learning their languages. As you take to the stars you’ll encounter pirates, be caught up in massive space battles between huge fleets, meet friendly traders, or simply drift alone through seemingly endless open space.

No Man’s Sky will have multiplayer of some kind and all players will be in the universe together but everyone will be quite spread out when they start so it’s not likely you’ll be running into anyone early in the game. Hello Games has been a bit vague as to how exactly multiplayer will work so while it’s widely assumed that you’ll starting running into more players as you get closer to the center of the universe it’s not quite clear if that will be the case. If you aren’t worried about finding the mysterious center then it’s quite likely that you’ll never see another player as long as you play the game considering the sheer size of the universe.

The Atlas

The focus of this game is exploration, so there is no campaign to complete. There isn’t even a way to “win” in No Man’s Sky, only the never-ending universe to be explored. However that’s not to say there is no story. Murray has continued to tease an overarching mystery, centered around the Atlas, which is the object we’ve seen in the games logo, and an antagonist of sorts that he calls “a malevolent force” as well as whatever lies at the fabled center of the universe. But even more than that there is a reason for your characters existence, why you’re there exploring the stars and tales of those that came before you, but the game isn’t going to just tell you this. You’ll have to search for it, finding clues and unraveling mysteries as you make your way through the expanse.

Of course exploration and survival require supplies and equipment though, as well as a capable ship for interstellar. That’s something that you’ll learn immediately since you’ll start on a planet with only a “lifepod” ship with no hyperdrive and some basic necessities. From there you’ll need to explore and gather resources to scrape together enough Units (the currency in No Man’s Sky) to purchase a ship of your own to begin exploring the universe. The basic equipment you’ll start out with is your suit and your multitool. Your suit provides protection from the elements, whether its acid rain or extreme cold, breathe underwater, and also do away with pesky gravity so that you’re not floating away with every step on some planets or getting crushed by your own weight on others. Your multitool does (big surprise here) multiple things, from a weapon (in case alien creatures get hostile), to a scanner, to a mining tool to extract resources. Murray has compared it to a Tricorder but sounds to me like a Tricorder strapped to a Phaser rifle, not that I’m complaining. You can also equip your character with a jetpack, but it appears you’ll have purchase one from a trading post before you start to flying to all those places that were just out of reach before. Your characters equipment can also be upgraded but only at trading posts on planets (this applies only to your personal equipment, not ship upgrades) so your suit could better protect your from the weather or you could upgrade your multitools mining capabilities.

Ships come in three classes Explorers, Fighters and Traders. Though it’s pretty self explanatory as to what each class of ship is for they won’t actually be labeled as a specific class. Instead each class will have specific enhancements suited for their individual role and these will give indication of what type of ship it is. Each ship will have between 1 to 3 of these enhancements and each of these can be upgraded to improve the performance of that component. For example fighters will be lighter, faster and more agile while traders will be slower and larger but their weapons will pack more of a punch. Exploration ships will have better stealth capabilities and more efficient hyperdrives to help them lay low and avoid (or run from) a fight which will be important since if you die, all the exploration data and resources you’ve collected since your last upload to the Atlas. That being said having your ship destroyed won’t necessarily cause you to meet an untimely demise, the ships cockpit is a lifepod like the one you start out with, so while you’ll have lost your ship and all it’s upgrades you can head down to a planet and start earning some Units for a new ride (and mourn the loss of your old one).

Buying and trading are done at outposts that you might come across while planetside or at space stations, which are quite common through out space (there are even some claims that these will be found in each system but that seems quite unlikely and I haven’t been able to find an official source for this). Units can be earned several ways such as uploading exploration data to the Atlas’ database from beacons found on planets, or by trading (if that wasn’t obvious already) as mentioned earlier and can be used to buy new ships or to upgrade your current one and to refuel your ship if you don’t feel like mining resources to refuel it yourself.

There is quite a lot more to No Man’s Sky and we’ll be looking closer at each aspect of the game in the coming weeks before launch.
